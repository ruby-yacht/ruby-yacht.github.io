6. THREE WITCHES

7. SCENE I. An open Place. Thunder and Lightning.

8. [Enter three Witches.]

9. FIRST WITCH.

10. When shall we three meet again?

11. In thunder, lightning, or in rain?

12. SECOND WITCH.

13. When the hurlyburly's done,

14. When the battle's lost and won.

15. THIRD WITCH.

16. That will be ere the set of sun.

17. FIRST WITCH.

18. Where the place?

19. SECOND WITCH.

20. Upon the heath.

21. THIRD WITCH.

22. There to meet with Macbeth.

23. ALL.

24. Fair is foul, and foul is fair:

25. Hover through the fog and filthy air.

26. [Witches vanish.]

27. BANQUO.

28. What bloody man is that? He can report,

29. As seemeth by his plight, of the revolt

30. The newest state.[ . . .] pronounce his present death,

31. And with his former title greet Macbeth.

32. What he hath lost, noble Macbeth hath won.

33. [Thunder. Enter the three Witches.]

34. [Drum within.]

35. THIRD WITCH.

36. A drum, a drum!

37. Macbeth doth come.

38. ALL.

39. The weird sisters, hand in hand,

40. Posters of the sea and land,

41. Thus do go about, about:

42. Thrice to thine, and thrice to mine,

43. And thrice again, to make up nine:--

44. Peace!--the charm's wound up.

45. [Enter Macbeth and Banquo.]

46. MACBETH.

47. So foul and fair a day I have not seen.

48. BANQUO.

49. How far is't call'd to Forres?--What are these

50. So wither'd, and so wild in their attire,

51. That look not like the inhabitants o' the earth,

52. And yet are on't?--Live you? or are you aught

53. That man may question?

54. MACBETH.

55. Speak, if you can;--what are you?

56. FIRST WITCH.

57. All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Glamis!

58. SECOND WITCH.

59. All hail, Macbeth! hail to thee, Thane of Cawdor!

60. THIRD WITCH.

61. All hail, Macbeth! that shalt be king hereafter!

62. BANQUO.

63. Good sir, why do you start; and seem to fear

64. Things that do sound so fair?-- I' the name of truth,

65. Are ye fantastical? [ . . . ] My noble partner

66. You greet with present grace and great prediction

67. Of noble having and of royal hope. . . .

68. If you can look into the seeds of time,

69. And say which grain will grow, and which will not,

70. Speak then to me, who neither beg nor fear

71. Your favors nor your hate.

72. FIRST WITCH.

73. Hail!

74. SECOND WITCH.

75. Hail!

76. THIRD WITCH.

77. Hail!

78. FIRST WITCH.

79. Lesser than Macbeth, and greater.

80. SECOND WITCH.

81. Not so happy, yet much happier.

82. THIRD WITCH.

83. Thou shalt get kings, though thou be none:

84. So all hail, Macbeth and Banquo!

85. FIRST WITCH.

86. Banquo and Macbeth, all hail!

87. MACBETH.

88. Stay, you imperfect speakers. Say from whence

89. You owe this strange intelligence?

90. [Witches vanish.]

91. BANQUO.

92. The earth hath bubbles, as the water has,

93. And these are of them:--whither are they vanish'd?

94. MACBETH.

95. Your children shall be kings.

96. BANQUO.

97. You shall be king.

98. MACBETH

99. Do you not hope your children shall be kings?

100. BANQUO.

101. 'Tis strange: oftentimes to win us to our harm,

102. The instruments of darkness tell us truths;

103. Win us with honest trifles, to betray's

104. In deepest consequence.--

105. MACBETH.

106. [Aside.] This supernatural soliciting

107. Cannot be ill; cannot be good:--if ill,

108. Why hath it given me earnest of success,

109. Commencing in a truth? I am Thane of Cawdor:

110. If good, why do I yield to that suggestion

111. Whose horrid image doth unfix my hair,

112. And make my seated heart knock at my ribs,

113. Against the use of nature? Present fears

114. Are less than horrible imaginings:

115. My thought, whose murder yet is but fantastical,

116. Shakes so my single state of mind, that function

117. Is smother'd in surmise; and nothing is

118. But what is not.

119. SCENE . Inverness. A Room in Macbeth's Castle.

120. LADY MACBETH.

121. Glamis thou art, and Cawdor; and shalt be

122. What thou art promis'd; yet do I fear thy nature;

123. It is too full o' the milk of human kindness

124. To catch the nearest way: " Hie thee hither,

125. That I may pour my spirits in thine ear.

126. The raven himself is hoarse

127. That croaks the fatal entrance of Duncan

128. Under my battlements. Come, you spirits

129. That tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here;

130. And fill me, from the crown to the toe, top-full

131. Of direst cruelty! Come to my woman's breasts,

132. And take my milk for gall, you murdering ministers,

133. Wherever in your sightless substances

134. You wait on nature's mischief! Come, thick night,

135. And pall thee in the dunnest smoke of hell

136. That my keen knife see not the wound it makes

137. Nor heaven peep through the blanket of the dark

138. To cry, "Hold, hold!"

139. [Enter Macbeth.]

140. Great Glamis! Worthy Cawdor!

141. Greater than both, by the all-hail hereafter!

142. Thy letters have transported me beyond

143. This ignorant present, and I feel now

144. The future in the instant.

145. MACBETH.

146. My dearest love,

147. Duncan comes here tonight.

148. LADY MACBETH.

149. And when goes hence?

150. MACBETH.

151. To-morrow,--as he purposes.

152. LADY MACBETH.

153. O, never

154. Shall sun that morrow see!

155. Your face, my thane, is as a book where men

156. May read strange matters:--to beguile the time,

157. Look like the time; bear welcome in your eye,

158. Your hand, your tongue: look like the innocent flower,

159. But be the serpent under't. He that's coming

160. Must be provided for: and you shall put

161. This night's great business into my despatch.

162. MACBETH.

163. We will speak further.

164. LADY MACBETH.

165. Only look up clear;

166. To alter favor ever is to fear:

167. Leave all the rest to me.

168. . . . .

169. BANQUO.

170. This guest of summer,

171. The temple-haunting martlet, does approve

172. By his lov'd mansionry, that the heaven's breath

173. Smells wooingly here: no jutty, frieze, buttress,

174. Nor coigne of vantage, but this bird hath made

175. His pendant bed and procreant cradle:

176. Where they most breed and haunt, I have observ'd

177. The air is delicate.

178. [Enter Macbeth.]

179. MACBETH.

180. If it were done when 'tis done, then 'twere well

181. It were done quickly. If the assassination

182. Could trammel up the consequence, and catch,

183. With his surcease, success; that but this blow

184. Might be the be-all and the end-all--here,

185. But here, upon this bank and shoal of time,--

186. We'd jump the life to come. . . Besides, this Duncan

187. Hath borne his faculties so meek, hath been

188. So clear in his great office, that his virtues

189. Will plead like angels, trumpet-tongued, against

190. The deep damnation of his taking-off:

191. And pity, like a naked new-born babe,

192. Striding the blast, or heaven's cherubin, hors'd

193. Upon the sightless couriers of the air,

194. Shall blow the horrid deed in every eye,

195. That tears shall drown the wind.--I have no spur

196. To prick the sides of my intent, but only

197. Vaulting ambition, which o'erleaps itself,

198. And falls on the other.

199. [Enter Lady Macbeth.]

200. How now! what news?

201. LADY MACBETH.

202. He has almost supp'd: why have you left the chamber?

203. MACBETH.

204. Hath he ask'd for me?

205. LADY MACBETH.

206. Know you not he has?

207. MACBETH.

208. We will proceed no further in this business:

209. He hath honour'd me of late; and I have bought

210. Golden opinions from all sorts of people,

211. Which would be worn now in their newest gloss,

212. Not cast aside so soon.

213. LADY MACBETH.

214. Was the hope drunk

215. Wherein you dress'd yourself? hath it slept since?

216. And wakes it now, to look so green and pale

217. At what it did so freely? From this time

218. Such I account thy love. Art thou afeard

219. To be the same in thine own act and valor

220. As thou art in desire?

221. MACBETH.

222. Pr'ythee, peace!

223. I dare do all that may become a man;

224. Who dares do more is none.

225. LADY MACBETH.

226. What beast was't, then,

227. That made you break this enterprise to me?

228. When you durst do it, then you were a man;

229. . . . . I have given suck, and know

230. How tender 'tis to love the babe that milks me:

231. I would, while it was smiling in my face,

232. Have pluck'd my nipple from his boneless gums

233. And dash'd the brains out, had I so sworn as you

234. Have done to this.

235. MACBETH.

236. If we should fail?

237. LADY MACBETH.

238. We fail!

239. But screw your courage to the sticking-place,

240. And we'll not fail.

241. MACBETH.

242. I am settled, and bend up

243. Each corporal agent to this terrible feat.

244. Away, and mock the time with fairest show:

245. False face must hide what the false heart doth know.

246. [Exeunt.]

247. BANQUO.

248. A heavy summons lies like lead upon me,

249. And yet I would not sleep:--merciful powers,

250. Restrain in me the cursed thoughts that nature

251. Gives way to in repose!--

252. Who's there?

253. MACBETH.

254. A friend.

255. BANQUO.

256. All's well.

257. I dreamt last night of the three weird sisters:

258. To you they have show'd some truth.

259. MACBETH.

260. I think not of them:

261. Yet, when we can entreat an hour to serve,

262. We would spend it in some words upon that business,

263. If you would grant the time.

264. BANQUO.

265. At your kind'st leisure.

266. MACBETH.

267. If you shall cleave to my consent,--when 'tis,

268. It shall make honor for you.

269. BANQUO.

270. So I lose none

271. In seeking to augment it, but still keep

272. My bosom franchis'd, and allegiance clear,

273. I shall be counsell'd.

274. MACBETH.

275. Good repose the while!

276. BANQUO.

277. Thanks, sir: the like to you!

278. MACBETH

279. Is this a dagger which I see before me,

280. The handle toward my hand? Come, let me clutch thee:--

281. I have thee not, and yet I see thee still.

282. Art thou not, fatal vision, sensible

283. To feeling as to sight? or art thou but

284. A dagger of the mind, a false creation,

285. Proceeding from the heat-oppressed brain?

286. Mine eyes are made the fools o' the other senses,

287. Or else worth all the rest: I see thee still;

288. And on thy blade and dudgeon gouts of blood,

289. Which was not so before.--There's no such thing:

290. It is the bloody business which informs

291. Thus to mine eyes.--Now o'er the one half-world

292. Nature seems dead, and wicked dreams abuse

293. The curtain'd sleep; [. . . ] Thou sure and firm-set earth,

294. Hear not my steps, which way they walk, for fear

295. Thy very stones prate of my whereabout,

296. And take the present horror from the time,

297. Which now suits with it.--Whiles I threat, he lives;

298. Words to the heat of deeds too cold breath gives.

299. [A bell rings.]

300. I go, and it is done; the bell invites me.

301. Hear it not, Duncan, for it is a knell

302. That summons thee to heaven or to hell.

303. [Exit.]

304. [Enter Lady Macbeth.]

305. LADY MACBETH.

306. That which hath made them drunk hath made me bold:

307. What hath quench'd them hath given me fire.--Hark!--Peace!

308. It was the owl that shriek'd, the fatal bellman,

309. Which gives the stern'st good night. He is about it:

310. The doors are open--

311. MACBETH.

312. [Within.] Who's there?--what, ho!

313. [Re-enter Macbeth.]

314. MACBETH.

315. I have done the deed.--Didst thou not hear a noise?

316. LADY MACBETH.

317. I heard the owl scream and the crickets cry.

318. Did not you speak?

319. MACBETH.

320. When?

321. LADY MACBETH.

322. Now.

323. MACBETH.

324. As I descended?

325. LADY MACBETH.

326. Ay.

327. MACBETH. [Looking on his hands.]

328. This is a sorry sight.

329. LADY MACBETH.

330. A foolish thought, to say a sorry sight.

331. These deeds must not be thought

332. After these ways; so, it will make us mad.

333. MACBETH.

334. I heard a voice cry, "Sleep no more!

335. Macbeth does murder sleep,"

336. LADY MACBETH.

337. Who was it that thus cried? Why, worthy thane,

338. You do unbend your noble strength to think

339. So brainsickly of things.--Go get some water,

340. And wash this filthy witness from your hand.--

341. MACBETH.

342. I'll go no more:

343. I am afraid to think what I have done;

344. Look on't again I dare not.

345. LADY MACBETH.

346. Infirm of purpose! The sleeping and the dead

347. Are but as pictures: 'tis the eye of childhood

348. That fears a painted devil.

349. [Exit. Knocking within.]

350. MACBETH.

351. Whence is that knocking?

352. How is't with me, when every noise appals me?

353. What hands are here? Ha, they pluck out mine eyes!

354. Will all great Neptune's ocean wash this blood

355. Clean from my hand? No; this my hand will rather

356. The multitudinous seas incarnadine,

357. Making the green one red.

358. LADY MACBETH.

359. A little water clears us of this deed:

360. How easy is it then! [ . . . ] be not lost

361. So poorly in your thoughts.

362. MACBETH.

363. To know my deed, 'twere best not know myself. [Knocking within.]

364. Wake Duncan with thy knocking! I would thou couldst!

365. [Exeunt.]

366. SCENE. Another Room in the Palace.

367. [Enter Lady Macbeth]

368. LADY MACBETH.

369. Naught's had, all's spent,

370. Where our desire is got without content:

371. 'Tis safer to be that which we destroy,

372. Than, by destruction, dwell in doubtful joy.

373. MACBETH.

374. We have scotch'd the snake, not kill'd it;

375. She'll close, and be herself; whilst our poor malice

376. Remains in danger of her former tooth.

377. But let the frame of things disjoint,

378. Both the worlds suffer,

379. Ere we will eat our meal in fear, and sleep

380. In the affliction of these terrible dreams

381. That shake us nightly: better be with the dead,

382. Whom we, to gain our peace, have sent to peace,

383. Than on the torture of the mind to lie

384. In restless ecstasy. Duncan is in his grave;

385. After life's fitful fever he sleeps well;

386. LADY MACBETH.

387. Come on;

388. Gently my lord, sleek o'er your rugged looks;

389. Be bright and jovial 'mong your guests to-night.

390. MACBETH.

391. So shall I, love; and so, I pray, be you:

392. Let your remembrance apply to Banquo;

393. Present him eminence, both with eye and tongue:

394. Unsafe the while, that we

395. Must lave our honors in these flattering streams;

396. And make our faces vizards to our hearts,

397. Disguising what they are.

398. LADY MACBETH.

399. You must leave this.

400. MACBETH.

401. O, full of scorpions is my mind, dear wife!

402. LADY MACBETH.

403. What's to be done?

404. MACBETH.

405. Be innocent of the knowledge, dearest chuck,

406. Till thou applaud the deed. Come, seeling night,

407. Scarf up the tender eye of pitiful day;

408. And with thy bloody and invisible hand

409. Cancel and tear to pieces that great bond

410. Which keeps me pale!--Light thickens; and the crow

411. Makes wing to the rooky wood:

412. Good things of day begin to droop and drowse;

413. Whiles night's black agents to their preys do rouse.--

414. Thou marvell'st at my words: but hold thee still;

415. Things bad begun make strong themselves by ill:

416. [Exeunt.]

417. MACBETH.

418. It will have blood; they say, blood will have blood:

419. Stones have been known to move, and trees to speak;

420. Augurs, and understood relations, have

421. By magot-pies, and choughs, and rooks, brought forth

422. The secret'st man of blood.--What is the night?

423. LADY MACBETH.

424. Almost at odds with morning, which is which.

425. MACBETH.

426. All causes shall give way: I am in blood

427. Step't in so far that, should I wade no more,

428. Returning were as tedious as go o'er:

429. Strange things I have in head, that will to hand;

430. Which must be acted ere they may be scann'd.

431. LADY MACBETH.

432. You lack the season of all natures, sleep.

433. MACBETH.

434. Come, we'll to sleep. My strange and self-abuse

435. Is the initiate fear that wants hard use:--

436. We are yet but young in deed.

437. [Exeunt.]

438. SCENE I. A dark Cave. In the middle, a Caldron Boiling.

439. ALL.

440. Double, double, toil and trouble;

441. Fire, burn; and caldron, bubble.

442. SECOND WITCH.

443. Cool it with a baboon's blood,

444. Then the charm is firm and good.

445. SECOND WITCH.

446. By the pricking of my thumbs,

447. Something wicked this way comes:--

448. Open, locks, whoever knocks!

449. [Enter Macbeth.]

450. MACBETH.

451. How now, you secret, black, and midnight hags!

452. What is't you do?

453. ALL.

454. A deed without a name.

455. MACBETH.

456. I conjure you, by that which you profess,--

457. Howe'er you come to know it,--answer me:

458. Though you untie the winds, and let them fight

459. Against the churches; though the yesty waves

460. Confound and swallow navigation up;

461. Though bladed corn be lodg'd, and trees blown down;

462. Though castles topple on their warders' heads;

463. Though palaces and pyramids do slope

464. Their heads to their foundations; though the treasure

465. Of nature's germins tumble all together,

466. Even till destruction sicken,--answer me

467. To what I ask you.

468. ALL.

469. Seek to know no more.

470. MACBETH.

471. I will be satisfied: deny me this,

472. And an eternal curse fall on you! Let me know:

473. FIRST WITCH.

474. Show!

475. SECOND WITCH.

476. Show!

477. THIRD WITCH.

478. Show!

479. ALL.

480. Show his eyes, and grieve his heart;

481. Come like shadows, so depart!

482. [Banquo appears.]

483. MACBETH.

484. Thou are too like the spirit of Banquo; down!

485. Thy crown does sear mine eyeballs:--.--Filthy hags!

486. Why do you show me this? --Start, eyes!

487. What, will the line stretch out to the crack of doom?

488. Horrible sight!--Now I see 'tis true;

489. For the blood-bolter'd Banquo smiles upon me,

490. And points at them for his.--What! is this so?

491. FIRST WITCH.

492. Ay, sir, all this is so:--but why

493. Stands Macbeth thus amazedly?--

494. Come,sisters, cheer we up his sprites,

495. And show the best of our delights;

496. I'll charm the air to give a sound,

497. While you perform your antic round;

498. That this great king may kindly say,

499. Our duties did his welcome pay.

500. [Music. The Witches dance, and then vanish.]

501. MACBETH.

502. Where are they? Gone?--Let this pernicious hour

503. Stand aye accursed in the calendar!--

504. SCENE I. Dunsinane. A Room in the Castle.

505. [Enter Lady Macbeth, with a taper.]

506. LADY MACBETH.

507. Yet here's a spot--

508. Out, damned spot! out, I say!-- One; two; why, then 'tis

509. time to do't ;--Hell is murky!--Fie, my lord, fie! a soldier,

510. and afeard? What need we fear who knows it, when none can

511. call our power to account?--Yet who would have thought

512. the man to have had so much blood in him?

513. . . . .

514. The Thane of Fife had a wife; where is she now?--What,

515. will these hands ne'er be clean? No more o' that, my lord, no

516. more o' that: you mar all with this starting.

517. . . . .

518. Here's the smell of the blood still: all the perfumes

519. of Arabia will not sweeten this little hand. Oh, oh, oh!

520. . . . .

521. Wash your hands, put on your nightgown; look not so

522. pale:--I tell you yet again, Banquo's buried; he cannot come

523. out on's grave.

524. . . . .

525. To bed, to bed; there's knocking at the gate: come, come, come,

526. come, give me your hand: what's done cannot be undone:

i. to bed, to bed, to bed.

527. SCENE. Dunsinane. Within the castle.

528. [A cry of women within.]

529. MACBETH.

530. What is that noise?

531. MACBETH.

532. I have almost forgot the taste of fears:

533. The time has been, my senses would have cool'd

534. To hear a night-shriek; and my fell of hair

535. Would at a dismal treatise rouse and stir

536. As life were in't: I have supp'd full with horrors;

537. Direness, familiar to my slaught'rous thoughts,

538. Cannot once start me.

539. Wherefore was that cry?

540. WITCH.

541. The queen, my lord, is dead.

542. MACBETH.

543. She should have died hereafter;

544. There would have been a time for such a word.--

545. To-morrow, and to-morrow, and to-morrow,

546. Creeps in this petty pace from day to day,

547. To the last syllable of recorded time;

548. And all our yesterdays have lighted fools

549. The way to dusty death. Out, out, brief candle!

550. Life's but a walking shadow; a poor player,

551. That struts and frets his hour upon the stage,

552. And then is heard no more: it is a tale

553. Told by an idiot, full of sound and fury,

554. Signifying nothing.
